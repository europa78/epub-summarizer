# PAUL'S BOUTIQUE (1989)

---

## "Hey Ladies"

Few tracks in the history of hip-hop announce themselves with quite the same swagger and sonic density as "Hey Ladies," the Beastie Boys' ode to romantic misadventure and self-congratulatory cool from their landmark 1989 album *Paul's Boutique*. Produced by the Dust Brothers (Matt Dike and Mike Simpson) alongside the Beastie Boys themselves, "Hey Ladies" is less a song in the conventional sense than a collage — a meticulously assembled mosaic of funk, soul, rock, hip-hop, and pop that somehow coheres into something utterly its own. To understand the track is to understand the samples that constitute its architecture, each one chosen not merely for its sonic utility but for what it carries culturally, historically, and emotionally.

The foundation of "Hey Ladies" is built, in large part, on the bones of the Commodores' "Machine Gun," the 1974 funk instrumental that established Lionel Richie's pre-pop outfit as one of the most potent groove machines of the decade. The Dust Brothers reach into "Machine Gun" for multiple elements — its guitar licks, its rhythmic skeleton, its overall feel of rolling, slow-burning cool. The Commodores track is itself a kind of orchestral funk, thick with brass and rhythm, and by threading it through "Hey Ladies," the producers anchor the track in a tradition of Black American funk that is simultaneously celebratory and authoritative. It gives the song a sense of weight that balances the Beasties' often goofball lyricism, ensuring that even when the trio is being deliberately absurd, the music beneath them carries real historical gravitas.

Layered against this is the drum work lifted from Jeanette "Lady" Day's "Come Let Me Love You (Instrumental)," a deeply obscure piece of Southern soul that represents the kind of crate-digging archaeology for which the Dust Brothers were already becoming legendary. The choice of Lady Day's drums is telling: rather than reaching for the most obvious or well-known breakbeats, the producers sought out a rhythm that could sit in a track without announcing its own origin, a drum groove that felt simultaneously familiar and fresh. This is the genius of the *Paul's Boutique* approach — the samples are often chosen precisely because they are not immediately recognizable, which means the listener experiences the groove rather than the reference.

The cartoon interlude provided by Hoyt Curtin's theme from "Magilla Gorilla" is one of the track's most unexpected and delightful moves. Curtin was the house composer for Hanna-Barbera, and his work defined the sonic texture of an entire generation's Saturday morning experience. By lifting a vocal or lyrical element from that context and dropping it into "Hey Ladies," the Dust Brothers do something genuinely subversive: they collapse the distance between childhood cultural memory and adult hip-hop bravado, creating a moment of cognitive dissonance that is both funny and strangely moving. It speaks to the Beasties' unique position as artists who grew up consuming pop culture voraciously and who now turn that consumption into art.

The Bar-Kays' "Holy Ghost" contributes the track's hook and riff, and this choice is crucial to understanding "Hey Ladies" as a piece of funk history. The Bar-Kays, who achieved immortality partly through their tragic connection to the Otis Redding plane crash of 1967 (most of the original band perished), reinvented themselves in the 1970s and early 1980s as one of the tightest funk outfits working. "Holy Ghost" — from their 1979 album of the same name — is a shimmering, synth-and-guitar driven groove that carries the post-disco funk sound at its most refined. By using it as the hook anchor of "Hey Ladies," the producers ensure that the track has a melodic spine strong enough to support all the chaos happening around it.

World's Famous Supreme Team's "Hey DJ" provides vocal and lyrical elements that root the track in hip-hop's immediate past. The Supreme Team were connected to the nascent New York hip-hop scene of the early 1980s, and their presence here is part of *Paul's Boutique*'s larger project of historicizing hip-hop even as it extends the form. James Brown appears twice — through "Ain't It Funky Now" and "Funky President (People It's Bad)" — which is almost inevitable given that Brown's catalog forms the DNA of so much hip-hop sampling, but the choice of these specific tracks rather than the more commonly pillaged Brown recordings again signals the depth of the producers' research.

Sweet's "Ballroom Blitz" is perhaps the most genre-crossing sample in the track, a glam-rock warhorse from 1973 that brings a completely different cultural register into the mix. Where most of the other samples come from Black American music traditions, "Ballroom Blitz" is British rock at its most theatrical and bombastic. Its inclusion is consistent with the Beastie Boys' position as white artists deeply immersed in Black music who also carry their own rock and punk lineage. The sample bridges those worlds in a single gesture.

Cameo's "Shake Your Pants" and Roger's "So Ruff, So Tuff" contribute multiple elements that thicken the track's bottom end and add layers of electro-funk texture. Roger Troutman's vocoder-and-funk aesthetic, which defined so much of early 1980s electro-R&B, gives "Hey Ladies" some of its most distinctly synthetic-sounding moments, a reminder that the track exists at the intersection of live-instrument funk and the emerging electronic sounds of the decade. The P-Funk All Stars' "Pumpin' It Up" adds additional drum elements, connecting the track to Parliament-Funkadelic's cosmic-funk universe, while Zapp's "Doo Wa Ditty (Blow That Thing)" layers in more Roger Troutman-family drums.

Deep Purple's "Hush," Kool & the Gang's "Jungle Boogie," Afrika Bambaataa's "Jazzy Sensation (Bronx Version)," Kurtis Blow's "Party Time," and the World's Famous Supreme Team — each of these samples represents a node in a network of music history that spans hard rock, early hip-hop, Bronx-originating electro, and classic party rap. Bambaataa's presence is especially significant: as the architect of electro-funk and one of hip-hop's founding fathers, his "Jazzy Sensation" brings the genre's own mythology into the mix, making "Hey Ladies" in part a song about hip-hop's history as well as a participant in it.

What all of these samples collectively produce is a track that is constantly moving, constantly shifting tonal register and cultural reference point, yet somehow never loses its central identity as a Beastie Boys song. The genius of "Hey Ladies" is that despite being assembled from sixteen distinct sources spanning three decades and multiple genres, it sounds inevitable — like this is precisely how these fragments were always meant to coexist. The samples don't just provide sonic raw material; they carry the weight of the traditions they come from, and by forcing those traditions into conversation, "Hey Ladies" becomes a kind of compressed history of popular music filtered through three young men from New York with exceptional taste and a genuine love for the records they were working with. The track works as a party song, as a critical statement about music history, and as a technical achievement in sampling composition — all simultaneously, all without breaking a sweat.

## "Shake Your Rump"

"Shake Your Rump" opens *Paul's Boutique* with one of the most kinetically thrilling minute-and-a-half runs in the history of recorded music, a rapid-fire volley of verses, beats, and sound fragments that essentially announces the album's entire aesthetic program before the first song is even done. As the lead track, it had to accomplish something nearly impossible: establish that the Beastie Boys of *Paul's Boutique* were categorically different from the *Licensed to Ill* group that had preceded them, while also remaining recognizably, defiantly themselves. The sample architecture of "Shake Your Rump" is the primary vehicle for that transformation, and it rewards close examination precisely because the sources chosen reflect not just musical utility but a coherent philosophy about what hip-hop could and should sound like.

The rhythmic foundation of "Shake Your Rump" draws on three distinct drum sources, and the interplay between them is the first sign that something unusual is happening. Alphonse Mouzon's "Funky Snakefoot" provides one layer of percussion — Mouzon being a jazz drummer of considerable repute whose work with Weather Report and his own solo records established him as one of the most technically accomplished players of the fusion era. The choice of a jazz-fusion drummer as a rhythmic source is itself a statement: rather than reaching exclusively into the soul and funk canon for beats, the Dust Brothers are drawing on music that values rhythmic complexity and improvisation in ways that pure funk often doesn't. Mouzon's drums carry a looseness, an expressiveness, that gives "Shake Your Rump" its slightly unpredictable pulse.

Louis Bellson's "Super Mellow" provides another drum layer, and Bellson's biography is relevant here. A big-band jazz drummer who played with Duke Ellington and Benny Goodman, Bellson represented an even older tradition — the swing era, the bebop transition, the entire arc of jazz drumming from the 1940s forward. By lifting a drum element from Bellson and placing it inside a hip-hop track in 1989, the Dust Brothers are performing a kind of musical time travel, collapsing sixty years of drumming history into a single groove. Harvey Scales' "Dancing Room Only" adds a third percussive dimension, Scales being a Milwaukee-based soul and funk artist whose work is a testament to the regional richness of American Black music outside the major coastal markets.

The combination of these three drum sources — jazz-fusion, big-band jazz, and regional soul — creates a rhythmic bed that is at once eclectic and cohesive, a groove that feels both precise and organic. This is the technical miracle of "Shake Your Rump": the beats hit hard enough to function as a pure hip-hop track, but they carry within them the DNA of multiple decades and traditions of American rhythm music.

Against this rhythmic backdrop, the melodic and harmonic content comes primarily from Rose Royce, who appears three times in the sample credits — through "6 O'Clock DJ (Let's Rock)," "Born to Love You," and "Yo Yo." Rose Royce, the Los Angeles group best known for their work on the *Car Wash* soundtrack and their own string of late-1970s disco-soul singles, had a sound defined by the tension between orchestral sweetness and rhythmic drive. By drawing on multiple Rose Royce recordings, the producers don't just lift individual sounds but effectively reconstruct something of Rose Royce's overall sonic world, creating a dialogue between tracks that the original artists themselves never had. "6 O'Clock DJ" and "Born to Love You" contribute hook and riff elements, while "Yo Yo" provides the bass — and it's that bassline that gives "Shake Your Rump" much of its forward momentum, the low-end engine driving everything above it.

Ronnie Laws' "Tell Me Something Good" provides another hook and riff element. Laws, the Houston-born saxophonist and flautist who made some of the smoothest jazz-funk of the late 1970s, brings a West Coast cool to the proceedings that complements Rose Royce's similarly relaxed California energy. Together, these Los Angeles and Houston sources give "Shake Your Rump" a distinctly West Coast-inflected feel, which is notable given that the Beastie Boys were emphatically New York artists — a geographic dislocation that adds to the track's polyglot character.

Afrika Bambaataa's "Jazzy Sensation (Bronx Version)" appears here again, this time as a sound effect rather than a structural element, which speaks to the granular level at which the Dust Brothers were working. They weren't just sampling chunks of records; they were reaching into specific moments, specific sounds, and deploying them with surgical precision. Bambaataa's appearance — even in this minor role — is a nod back to hip-hop's foundational geography, the Bronx, which grounds the track's experimental eclecticism in the music's own roots.

The hip-hop history encoded in "Shake Your Rump" runs deep. Funky 4 + 1's "That's the Joint" — a significant early rap record from 1980 — provides multiple elements, making the Dust Brothers' debt to early hip-hop explicit. The Funky 4 + 1 were among the first crews to put female emcees in prominent roles, and "That's the Joint" was a foundational document of the Bronx rap scene. Sugarhill Gang's "8th Wonder" adds more hip-hop-origins material; the Sugarhill Gang, of course, were the group whose "Rapper's Delight" had introduced hip-hop to mainstream audiences in 1979, and their presence here is another act of historical acknowledgment. Afrika Bambaataa's "Unity (Pt. 2)" — a track explicitly about peace and cooperation — adds a lyrical/vocal element that carries political weight even in this highly compressed context.

The B-Boys' "Rock the House" contributes vocals and lyrics, while Foxy's "Get Off" — a 1978 funk track that is itself a study in groove efficiency — adds a vocal element that brings yet another strand of 1970s funk into the weave. Each of these additions thickens the track's historical consciousness without slowing its momentum.

What makes "Shake Your Rump" so remarkable as a piece of sampling art is the way it synthesizes these wildly disparate elements into something that sounds not patchwork but unified. The track moves at a pace that doesn't give the listener time to identify individual samples even if they know them all — it's designed to be experienced as a whole rather than anatomized. That tension between surface accessibility and deep complexity is perhaps the defining characteristic of *Paul's Boutique* as an album, and "Shake Your Rump" is its purest expression, a track where thirteen distinct sources are made to speak as one continuous, joyful, slightly chaotic voice.

## "Egg Man"

"Egg Man" is one of the strangest and most ambitious tracks on *Paul's Boutique*, which is saying something on an album that includes a seven-minute medley and a section that samples a cartoon about a gorilla. Where "Hey Ladies" deploys its samples in service of party-rap bravado and "Shake Your Rump" uses them to build a kinetic groove showcase, "Egg Man" has something more unsettling on its mind. The track is ostensibly a narrative about throwing eggs at people — a juvenile premise if ever there was one — but the sample architecture transforms that premise into something approaching a short horror-comedy film, a piece of music that is simultaneously funny, creepy, propulsive, and culturally layered in ways that reveal themselves slowly over multiple listens.

The structural spine of "Egg Man" is built on Curtis Mayfield's "Superfly," the title track from the 1972 blaxploitation film soundtrack that remains one of the towering achievements of 1970s American music. Mayfield's "Superfly" is a song of extraordinary moral complexity — nominally the theme for a drug dealer, but infused with such ambivalence and melancholy that the result is closer to a lament than a celebration. The string arrangements, the wah-wah guitar, the floating, almost weightless quality of Mayfield's production — all of these elements are present in the samples drawn from it for "Egg Man," and they give the track an immediate sense of cinematic scale and emotional depth that its egg-throwing subject matter might not otherwise command. By placing the Beastie Boys' narrative over a Mayfield foundation, the producers are borrowing the gravity and cultural significance of the blaxploitation era's most sophisticated musical statement and applying it to a story that is, on its surface, about teenage mischief. The resulting irony honors the original by demonstrating how durable and powerful its musical language is.

Then comes Bernard Herrmann. The decision to sample "The Murder" from Alfred Hitchcock's *Psycho* — one of the most recognizable pieces of horror film scoring in cinema history, those shrieking, staccato strings that accompany the shower scene — is a move of considerable audacity and sophistication. Herrmann's score for *Psycho* is not merely background music; it is a participant in the film's violence, an amplification of psychological terror. By lifting multiple elements from it for "Egg Man," the Dust Brothers are importing an entire tradition of cinematic menace, collapsing the distance between avant-garde concert music, horror film scoring, and hip-hop production. The effect is jarring in the best possible sense: suddenly the egg man of the title carries a faint whiff of the unhinged, of Hitchcockian obsession. The humor remains, but it becomes slightly unnerving humor.

John Williams' "Main Title (Theme from Jaws)" performs a similar function, though with different connotations. Where Herrmann's strings bring psychological terror, Williams' two-note motif carries cultural ubiquity — there is perhaps no piece of film music more immediately recognizable to an American audience of the late 1980s than the Jaws theme. By deploying it in "Egg Man," the producers are playing with the audience's conditioned responses: that two-note phrase, even lifted out of its original context, cannot help but trigger associations with threat and the approach of danger. Applied to the egg-throwing narrative, it creates a wildly disproportionate emotional response — which is the joke, and also something more than the joke.

Sly & the Family Stone's "Dance to the Music" provides the track's primary drum element, and the choice is loaded with significance. "Dance to the Music," from 1968, was one of the foundational documents of what would become funk — a record that broke down the conventional pop-song arrangement and made rhythm the central subject of the music. Sly Stone's influence on virtually every subsequent form of Black American music is incalculable, and by building "Egg Man" on a "Dance to the Music" break, the producers connect the track to that foundational moment.

Tower of Power's "Drop It in the Slot" contributes multiple elements, bringing the Bay Area funk collective's extraordinarily tight horn arrangements and rhythmic precision into the mix. Lightnin' Rod's "Sport" provides additional drum elements; Lightnin' Rod (Jalal Mansur Nuriddin of The Last Poets) was a proto-rapper whose spoken-word recordings over funk tracks in the early 1970s constitute some of the most direct precursors to hip-hop ever committed to record. His appearance in "Egg Man"'s drum credits is another act of genre genealogy.

Elvis Costello's "Pump It Up" is one of the track's most unexpected inclusions. Costello's 1978 track — a driving piece of post-punk/new wave — brings a completely different cultural register into "Egg Man"'s already eclectic space. The track's punk-adjacent aggression adds a raw, abrasive edge that cuts against both the Mayfield smoothness and the cinematic grandeur of the film score samples. It also connects to the Beastie Boys' own punk roots — before they were rappers, they were a hardcore punk band, and the Costello sample is a thread back to that origin.

The hip-hop intertexts are rich and deliberate. Public Enemy appear twice — through "Bring the Noise" and "You're Gonna Get Yours" — in vocal and lyrical capacities that constitute a remarkable act of peer-acknowledgment. In 1989, Public Enemy were hip-hop's most politically charged and sonically confrontational group; their inclusion in "Egg Man" is the Beasties declaring their artistic kinship and mutual respect. The Beastie Boys sample themselves, drawing on "Egg Raid on Mojo" from their earliest recordings — a call-back to their own prehistory that mirrors the track's broader project of historical excavation. And then there is the Dr. Seuss element: vocal material drawn from "Green Eggs and Ham" that is deployed with the same levity and genuine love that animated the Magilla Gorilla sample in "Hey Ladies."

What "Egg Man" ultimately demonstrates, and what makes it the most ambitious track on *Paul's Boutique*, is that sampling can function not just as rhythm and hook construction but as genuine narrative and tonal modulation. The Herrmann and Williams cues don't just provide sonic material — they create emotional and cinematic context. The Mayfield samples don't just ground the track rhythmically — they invest it with a history and moral complexity that the surface narrative doesn't require but that makes the whole thing resonate at a deeper frequency. "Egg Man" is a comedy that is genuinely unsettling, a hip-hop track that thinks like a film, and a juvenile story that somehow carries the weight of fifty years of American music history in its approximately three-minute runtime.

## "Car Thief"

Few tracks on *Paul's Boutique* capture the album's restless, magpie-brained genius quite like "Car Thief," a song that functions less like a conventional hip-hop track and more like a guided tour through a record crate that somehow caught fire and reassembled itself mid-burn. Produced by the Dust Brothers alongside Beastie Boys member Mike D, "Car Thief" represents the album's collage aesthetic at its most densely layered, stitching together funk, folk-rock, go-go, and the counterculture's own mythological vocabulary into something wholly new.

The track's spine is built in large part from Funk Factory's "Rien Ne Va Plus," a deep, relatively obscure European funk record whose title translates roughly to "no more bets" — a phrase croupiers use when roulette wheels stop spinning. The choice is almost metaphorical: "Car Thief" is itself a kind of roulette, spinning through genres and eras with no predictable landing spot. Funk Factory operated in that late-1970s pocket where Continental and American funk traditions were blurring into each other, and their production style had a slightly wiry, mechanical quality that gives the track a gritty, almost industrial undertow. The Dust Brothers drew on multiple elements from "Rien Ne Va Plus," meaning they weren't simply looping a beat or a bassline but extracting and reassembling component parts — percussion accents, horn stabs, rhythmic textures — to build the compositional skeleton of "Car Thief."

Layered atop this is the drum contribution from Donovan's "Hurdy Gurdy Man," the 1968 psychedelic folk-rock masterpiece that is itself one of the most sonically peculiar Top 5 hits in British chart history. The drums on "Hurdy Gurdy Man" — played by John Bonham, by some accounts — have a cavernous, almost prehistoric quality, a thunderous lurch that sounds like it's being played in a stone corridor rather than a studio. The Beastie Boys and the Dust Brothers had a documented fascination with the intersection between classic rock drumming and hip-hop rhythm, and the use of the "Hurdy Gurdy Man" drums here speaks to that obsession. There is something deeply right about dropping Donovan's spacey, mystical production into a hip-hop context: the original song's lysergic wobble translates surprisingly well into the late-1980s New York urban landscape.

The melodic hook arrives courtesy of The Jackson 5's "I'll Bet You," a track from the group's 1969 debut album *Diana Ross Presents the Jackson 5.* This is Motown at its most rawly energetic — before the Jackson 5 had been fully sanded and polished into pop perfection, "I'll Bet You" has a funky, almost aggressive quality that distinguishes it from the group's smoother subsequent hits. The riff or hook extracted from this song introduces a melodic identity to "Car Thief" that anchors the listener even as everything else is shifting beneath their feet.

Trouble Funk's "Drop the Bomb" arrives as one of the track's most politically charged inclusions. Trouble Funk were central figures in Washington D.C.'s go-go scene, a genre that developed in parallel with New York hip-hop but maintained its own distinct communal, call-and-response character. Go-go was and remains deeply embedded in D.C.'s Black community, a music of block parties and neighborhood solidarity, and "Drop the Bomb" is one of the genre's canonical texts — a sprawling, percussion-heavy groove that demands physical participation. The Dust Brothers drew multiple elements from this track, weaving go-go's infectious rhythmic sensibility into the track's already complex tapestry.

Perhaps the most conceptually audacious sample in "Car Thief" is the spoken word inclusion from Max Yasgur's "Address to the Crowd," the famous speech Yasgur — whose farm hosted Woodstock in August 1969 — delivered to the assembled festival audience. Yasgur was a conservative dairy farmer from upstate New York who made his land available to the counterculture at considerable personal and professional risk. Sampling Yasgur's voice in 1989 is a pointed act of historical reclamation — it's the Beastie Boys reaching back twenty years to the moment when youth culture last seemed to cohere around a shared utopian vision, and implicitly asking what happened to it. In the context of a song about car theft, hustling, and urban survival, Yasgur's earnest optimism functions as both tribute and irony, a reminder of a dream that the subsequent two decades had largely dismembered.

"Car Thief," for all its surface chaos, is ultimately a meditation on what gets taken, what gets left behind, and what, improbably, gets preserved in the grooves of a record.

## "Shadrach"

"Shadrach" is arguably the greatest achievement on *Paul's Boutique*, and that's a contested title on an album full of genuine landmarks. Named for one of the three Hebrew youths in the Book of Daniel who survived being thrown into Nebuchadnezzar's furnace, "Shadrach" builds its identity around an almost theological faith in the power of the groove — the idea that righteous funkiness is itself a kind of indestructibility. The track deploys ten distinct samples, drawing from a range of sources that spans twenty years of American popular music history, and the sheer scope of its ambition is matched only by how effortlessly the final product flows.

The track's emotional center begins with Sly & the Family Stone's "Loose Booty," from the 1974 album *Small Talk.* By 1974, Sly Stone was a different figure than the transcendent force who had made *Stand!* and *There's a Riot Goin' On* — "Loose Booty" comes from a period of dissolution and commercial decline, but there's still something raw and truthful in it. The vocal elements lifted from this track carry a quality of defiant pleasure, a kind of joy that has been tested and endured. The choice to begin with Sly Stone is, in itself, a statement about the Beastie Boys' artistic lineage — Sly's work was foundational to hip-hop in ways that are sometimes underappreciated, and invoking him explicitly is a form of gratitude and acknowledgment.

The rhythmic foundation is built most significantly on James Brown's "Funky Drummer," recorded in 1969 with drummer Clyde Stubblefield laying down what would become the most sampled drum break in recorded music history. Stubblefield's pattern on "Funky Drummer" has a quality of mechanical inevitability — it is precise without being robotic, deeply human in its micro-fluctuations while maintaining a lockstep authority that makes it almost impossible to resist. The decision to anchor so musically and conceptually ambitious a track on this particular drum pattern is itself a statement of values, an alignment with a specific tradition of Black American rhythmic genius.

The track further draws drums from Black Oak Arkansas's "Hot and Nasty," a 1971 Southern rock deep cut. Layering a Southern rock drum element with the precision of Clyde Stubblefield creates a productive tension, a sense that the track is being held together by force of will rather than mathematical inevitability.

Ballin' Jack's "Never Let 'Em Say" provides multiple elements and represents one of the deeper cuts in the entire *Paul's Boutique* sample catalog. Ballin' Jack were a Seattle-based jazz-funk group of the early 1970s whose limited commercial success has never prevented them from being regarded as a treasure by serious record collectors. Their sound has a particular lushness, a sophisticated harmonic vocabulary that distinguishes it from rougher funk contemporaries.

Rose Royce's "Do Your Dance," from the 1977 film *Car Wash*, brings the production vocabulary of late-disco into the mix. Multiple elements are used, suggesting the Dust Brothers found the track's full sonic architecture useful rather than just a particular hook or groove.

The vocal sample from The Fatback Band's "King Tim III (Personality Jock)" carries enormous historical weight. Released in 1979 — slightly before "Rapper's Delight" and sometimes cited as the actual first commercially released rap record — "King Tim III" represents hip-hop's own original moment, the genre's emergence as a recorded rather than purely live phenomenon. Sampling it in "Shadrach" is an act of self-conscious historical positioning, the Beastie Boys reaching back to the genre's literal beginning to establish their place in a tradition.

AC/DC's "For Those About to Rock (We Salute You)" is perhaps the most audaciously incongruous sample in "Shadrach." The "For Those About to Rock" vocal element drops into "Shadrach" like a cannon shot, a moment of genre collision that is both genuinely funny and genuinely illuminating. The Beastie Boys had always positioned themselves at the intersection of hip-hop and rock, and sampling AC/DC alongside James Brown and Sly Stone is an argument about cultural geography: these things exist in the same world and can be made to speak to each other.

Trouble Funk's "Say What?" contributes more go-go energy, while Funky 4 + 1's "That's the Joint" and Sugarhill Gang's "Sugarhill Groove" complete the track's historiographical project. By sampling both an early Bronx hip-hop group and the crew whose "Rapper's Delight" was hip-hop's first mainstream breakthrough, "Shadrach" positions itself in explicit dialogue with hip-hop's founding moment. "Shadrach" may be the densest, most layered argument in favor of sampling as a genuine artistic practice ever committed to tape.

## "Johnny Ryall"

"Johnny Ryall" is the most character-driven track on *Paul's Boutique*, a song built around the portrait of a fictional homeless country singer who haunts a New York City block with the remnants of some former musical ambition. The song operates as a vignette, a short story in hip-hop form, and its ten samples are deployed not just for rhythmic or sonic effect but to build the specific emotional and cultural atmosphere of Johnny's world. This is sampling as scene-setting, as characterization — each source adds a texture that contributes to the portrait of a man living between musical worlds, between past and present.

The drumming foundation begins with Paul McCartney's "Momma Miss America," an instrumental from the 1970 album *McCartney* — his first solo record, recorded at home with primitive equipment after the Beatles' disintegration. "Momma Miss America" has a ragged, homemade quality that McCartney himself has described as deliberately lo-fi; it sounds like music being made in private, for private reasons, without concern for commercial polish. Using McCartney's drums in "Johnny Ryall" is a quietly poignant choice: the home-recorded intimacy of the source material rhymes with Johnny's own outsider status, his existence at the margins of the music industry.

Pink Floyd's "One of These Days" contributes a sound effect that introduces a note of science fiction into the proceedings. "One of These Days" is one of Pink Floyd's most aggressive and alien-sounding recordings, a piece that evokes vast, cold spaces and mechanical menace. Its appearance in "Johnny Ryall" is disorienting in the best possible sense: it momentarily pulls the track into a different sonic universe, a reminder that New York City itself is a strange and inhuman environment when viewed from certain angles.

Donny Hathaway's "Magnificent Sanctuary Band" provides drums that carry an almost sacred quality. Hathaway's 1970 recording of this spiritual was one of his most overtly religious works. The drums from this track have a gospel-adjacent feel, a sense of purposeful reverence. In the context of "Johnny Ryall," drawing on Hathaway's spiritual drumming is an act of compassion toward the song's subject — it dignifies Johnny's marginal existence with a suggestion that something worthwhile persists in him.

David Bromberg's "Sharon" provides multiple elements and situates the track's country-folk dimension. Bromberg was a supremely gifted guitarist and singer whose work ranged across folk, country, blues, and bluegrass with a musicologist's command. "Sharon" brings the acoustic, Americana side of his catalog into the mix, establishing that Johnny's musical identity is not just a cultural signifier but a specific sound-world rooted in American vernacular music that runs parallel to and intersects with the hip-hop tradition.

Jean Knight's "Mr. Big Stuff" vocal sample introduces a complex irony. Knight's 1971 Southern soul classic is addressed to someone who considers himself too important for ordinary people. Sampling it in a song about a homeless man is a wry reversal of the original's logic: Johnny Ryall is the anti-"Big Stuff," a man stripped of pretension by circumstance.

DJ Grand Wizard Theodore's "Military Cut — Scratch Mix" is one of the track's most self-referential inclusions. Grand Wizard Theodore is widely credited with inventing the scratch technique — that fundamental hip-hop turntable gesture — and his presence here is both a tribute and a claim to heritage.

The sampling of Kurtis Blow's "AJ Scratch" and the self-referential reuse of the Beastie Boys' own records — both "(You Gotta) Fight for Your Right (To Party!)" and "The New Style" — creates the most unusual dimension of "Johnny Ryall's" sample architecture. By including their own previous work among the sources, the Beastie Boys are positioning themselves inside the track's genealogy rather than outside it, acknowledging that they too are products of a specific musical moment, that their own recordings are already becoming the raw material from which new things can be built.

"Johnny Ryall" may be the most personal track on *Paul's Boutique* precisely because it buries that personal dimension so deeply inside layers of musical references that it can only be found by those willing to do the excavation.

## "High Plains Drifter"

In the vast, genre-defying sonic landscape of *Paul's Boutique*, "High Plains Drifter" stands as one of the album's most cinematically conceived tracks. The title itself — borrowed from the Clint Eastwood spaghetti-western-adjacent film — signals the Beastie Boys' and the Dust Brothers' intent: this is a song that wants to feel like a movie, sprawling and strange, pulling its audience through a collage of American music history the way a fever dream pulls you through half-remembered memories.

The sonic foundation is built primarily on the Eagles' "Those Shoes," from the 1979 album *The Long Run*. This is, on its surface, an almost comically incongruous choice. The Eagles are the band most associated with polished, palm-tree California rock. "Those Shoes" is particularly angular by their standards, driven by a processed, almost robotic synthesizer guitar riff run through an MXR Flanger to give it a queasy, warbly edge. It is, in the context of the Eagles' catalog, already a slightly destabilized piece of work — a song about materialism and surface-level desire dressed up in a sound that feels vaguely machine-like. The Dust Brothers seized on precisely that destabilized quality. By looping and manipulating multiple elements from "Those Shoes," they transformed a slick rock track into something that feels genuinely disorienting. In a hip-hop context in 1989, using the Eagles was not a winking irony — it was a genuine provocation, a challenge to genre orthodoxy.

The Band's "Up on Cripple Creek" — one of Robbie Robertson's most affectionately rustic compositions, featuring Levon Helm's wonderfully loose, narrative vocal delivery — contributes vocal and lyrical fragments. The Band were always a group out of time, playing a kind of music that felt simultaneously ancient and immediate, rooted in the Mississippi Delta and the Canadian prairie at once. Pulling Helm's earthy, drawling delivery into the mix gives "High Plains Drifter" a sense of mythological Americana that sits perfectly alongside the Clint Eastwood imagery of the title.

The Fatback Band's "Put Your Love (In My Tender Care)" provides another layer of vocal texture. The Fatback Band were one of the true architects of funk's transition into disco, a Brooklyn group who were releasing records as early as 1970 and who, by many accounts, helped birth hip-hop itself with their 1979 single "King Tim III." Having their vocal fragments appear in a hip-hop context is less a collision than a homecoming.

The Ramones' "Suzy Is a Headbanger" and Loggins and Messina's "Your Mama Don't Dance" arrive as the track's most gleefully absurdist gestures. Taking vocal fragments from the Ramones — the purest, most deliberately stripped-down punk band that ever lived — and blending them with the soft-rock country-pop of Kenny Loggins and Jim Messina creates a kind of pop-cultural whiplash that is entirely deliberate. Putting the Ramones next to Loggins and Messina is a philosophical statement: the borders between "cool" and "uncool," between punk credibility and soft-rock, are arbitrary and worth demolishing.

And then there is the dialogue sample from *The Empire Strikes Back* — specifically the "Luke Meets Yoda" sequence. Yoda's strange, inverted syntax and his air of ancient, gnomic authority play beautifully against the track's self-consciously mythological Western imagery. The Force and the frontier become interchangeable mythologies, equally American in their appeal to manifest destiny and heroic individualism. The Beastie Boys are positioning themselves as gunslingers in a cosmic landscape, navigating a musical universe as vast and dangerous as the galaxy far, far away.

"High Plains Drifter" moves like a chase scene: urgent, unpredictable, always threatening to fly apart but never quite doing so. The result is a track that sounds like it could only have been made in 1989, in New York, by artists who had absorbed everything American music had ever produced and decided, gleefully, to set it all on fire at once.

## "What Comes Around"

"What Comes Around" is in many ways the most musically aggressive track on an already aggressive album. Where "High Plains Drifter" leans into a kind of lateral, panoramic strangeness, "What Comes Around" is a more vertical construction — it piles weight on weight, layering hard rock thunder against jazz sophistication against the bedrock of early hip-hop, until the whole structure groans under its own density.

The track's most immediately striking element is the drum sample from Led Zeppelin's "Moby Dick" — specifically the studio version from *Led Zeppelin II*, recorded in 1969. John Bonham was, by virtually universal critical consensus, the most powerful rock drummer of his generation, and "Moby Dick" exists primarily as a monument to that power. The studio version features Bonham playing with his bare hands at points, a technique that gave his already thunderous playing an extra tactile, almost violent quality. The sound of those drums — the way the kick drum seems to move actual air, the way the snare cracks with a sharpness that feels almost physical — was essentially unmatched in the rock era. When that sound is transplanted into a hip-hop track, something remarkable happens: it instantly changes the weight and gravity of the music around it. Hip-hop drum machines produce sounds that exist in a different dimension from Bonham's playing. They are crisp, precise, and often deliberately artificial. Bonham's drums are overwhelmingly, uncomfortably real. By building "What Comes Around" around that Bonham sample, the Beastie Boys and the Dust Brothers created a hip-hop track with genuine physical menace.

Gene Harris and the Three Sounds' "Put on Train" provides a striking counterpoint. Gene Harris was a jazz pianist of considerable sophistication, best known for a warm, blues-inflected approach that made him one of the most accessible jazz players of the 1960s and 70s. The effect of layering Harris's jazz warmth against Bonham's rock brutality is to create a track that occupies an impossible sonic space — simultaneously loose and crushing, simultaneously cool and hot.

Alice Cooper's "It's Hot Tonight" brings yet another dimension. Cooper's work was always melodramatically theatrical, and the hook or riff element sampled from "It's Hot Tonight" functions almost as a taunt within the track, a sneering interjection that adds an element of theatrical malevolence. Cooper's influence on the Beastie Boys is perhaps underappreciated: like them, he understood that performance could be simultaneously sincere and absurd.

The sample from the Mohawks' "The Champ" represents one of the more historically significant choices on the track. "The Champ," recorded by the British soul group the Mohawks in 1968, is one of the most sampled records in hip-hop history — its drum break and vocal call-and-response have appeared in hundreds of tracks across four decades. By invoking one of hip-hop's sacred texts within a track that also draws on Led Zeppelin and Gene Harris, the Beastie Boys are drawing a line between the music they're making and the deep roots of the tradition they're working in.

What "What Comes Around" achieves is a demonstration that heaviness in music is not a property of any single genre. Rock heaviness, jazz groove, soul call-and-response, hip-hop flow — all of these are different expressions of the same underlying impulse, which is to make music that the body cannot ignore.

## "B-Boy Bouillabaisse"

To describe "B-Boy Bouillabaisse" simply as a song is to fundamentally misrepresent what it is. Running to nearly twelve minutes in its complete form, the closing medley of *Paul's Boutique* is an act of musical architecture without precedent in hip-hop — a multi-movement work that draws on sixteen separately identifiable samples while managing to cohere into something that feels, astonishingly, unified. It is a bouillabaisse in the most literal culinary sense: a dish made from whatever the cook has at hand, the quality of which depends entirely on the cook's skill in making disparate ingredients speak to each other.

The track begins with the Isley Brothers' "That Lady" — specifically its iconic, wah-wah-saturated guitar hook, one of Ernie Isley's most electrifying performances and one of the defining sounds of funk-influenced R&B in the early 1970s. "That Lady" is a track about desire and longing, but Ernie Isley's guitar work transforms those emotions into something almost aggressive. Using this hook as an entry point announces that we are beginning in the realm of pure, physical groove. The Isley Brothers' hook also situates the track within a tradition of Black American funk and R&B that is the deepest root of hip-hop.

Idris Muhammad's "Loran's Dance" contributes another hook and riff element. Muhammad — born Leo Morris, he changed his name upon converting to Islam — was one of the great session drummers of his era, appearing on records by Lou Donaldson, Pharoah Sanders, and dozens of others. "Loran's Dance" is a showcase for his ability to create rhythms that are simultaneously precise and loose. Pairing Muhammad's material with the Isley Brothers' hook creates a rhythmic and melodic environment that feels both jazz-sophisticated and street-level immediate.

Johnny Cash's "Folsom Prison Blues" arrives as one of the track's most startling juxtapositions. Cash is the patron saint of a very specific kind of American outlaw mythology — the man in black, the convict's advocate. Using vocal and lyrical elements from "Folsom Prison Blues" in a hip-hop context in 1989 was a choice with multiple resonances. It reminds you that the mythology of American outlaw culture cuts across racial and regional lines, and it suggests that hip-hop's relationship to that mythology is more complicated and more historically rooted than simple genre analysis would suggest.

Jimi Hendrix's "Are You Experienced?" contributes a hook and riff element that functions as a kind of psychedelic disruption. Hendrix's work was always about the destabilization of expectation — his guitar playing took the vocabulary of the blues and exploded it into abstraction. Using "Are You Experienced?" as a source adds a layer of psychedelic flux to the medley.

The rhythm section of "B-Boy Bouillabaisse" draws on an extraordinary range of drum sources. The Crusaders' "The Well's Gone Dry" provides bass. Tower of Power's "Ebony Jam" brings drums from one of the tightest and most technically accomplished funk ensembles in the country. Then there is the single most sonically significant drum sample on the track: John Bonham's "When the Levee Breaks," from Led Zeppelin's untitled fourth album, recorded in the stairwell of Headley Grange with the microphones placed at the bottom of the staircase to capture the natural reverb. The result is a drum sound so massive, so physically overwhelming, that it remains to this day one of the most recognizable individual drum sounds ever recorded.

The Burundi Steiphenson Black "Burundi Black" drums add a completely different percussive dimension: recorded in Burundi and featuring the traditional royal drumming traditions of that country, "Burundi Black" was a 1971 recording that presented African ceremonial percussion to European and American audiences. Having Burundian ceremonial drums and John Bonham's Hampshire stairwell thunder occupy the same sonic space is the kind of global-historical collision that "B-Boy Bouillabaisse" deals in constantly and fearlessly.

The Southside Movement's "Save the World" and Trouble Funk's "Good to Go" both contribute multiple elements and anchor the track in funk and go-go music. Scotty's "Draw Your Brakes" brings a reggae dimension — Scotty was a Jamaican deejay who recorded in the early 1970s, and "Draw Your Brakes" is a classic example of the toasting tradition, a precursor to rap, in which a deejay speaks rhythmically over a riddim track. The connection between Jamaican sound system culture and American hip-hop is one of the foundational histories of the genre.

The Meters' "Hey Pocky A-Way" brings the deep, funky heart of New Orleans into the mix. The Meters were the house band of Allen Toussaint's recording empire and arguably the greatest funk band New Orleans ever produced. Kool & the Gang's "Let the Music Take Your Mind" adds another layer of early funk history. The Sweet's "Into the Night" provides drums from the glam-rock world.

Chic's "Good Times" and Malcolm McLaren's "Buffalo Gals" arrive as the track's most self-referential samples. "Good Times" is one of the great foundational documents of hip-hop — its bass line was looped by early hip-hop DJs and appears at the root of countless early rap records. McLaren's "Buffalo Gals," meanwhile, was one of the first mainstream pop records to incorporate hip-hop scratching and b-boy culture, a 1982 record that introduced many listeners to the aesthetic of the South Bronx.

"B-Boy Bouillabaisse" is, in the end, an argument about the nature of American music and the nature of hip-hop's relationship to it. All of this music — the funk and the jazz, the reggae and the rock, the country and the R&B, the African ceremonial and the British glam, the New Orleans second-line and the D.C. go-go — all of it belongs to the same continuous, expanding tradition of American popular music, and hip-hop is not separate from that tradition but is the form that most fully and honestly acknowledges the tradition's inherently mixed and borrowed and recombined nature.

## "The Sounds of Science"

There is a moment in the history of hip-hop production where the art of the sample transcended mere borrowing and became something closer to archaeology — a passionate excavation of the recorded past in service of an entirely new present. "The Sounds of Science," the sprawling, psychedelic centerpiece of *Paul's Boutique*, is arguably the finest single example of that transformation. Produced by the Dust Brothers, it is a track built almost entirely from the bones of other records, and yet it feels like nothing that had ever existed before. At its heart is one of the most audacious acts of sonic appropriation in pop music history: the wholesale dismantling and reconstruction of The Beatles' *Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band*.

To understand what the Dust Brothers and the Beastie Boys pulled off here, you have to appreciate what *Sgt. Pepper's* meant in 1989. This was not simply a beloved album. It was a cultural monument — the record that critics had for two decades treated as the most important pop music artifact ever created. Sampling it was not just a creative choice; it was a statement. It said that hip-hop production had arrived at a level of confidence and cultural authority where even the most untouchable works of the rock canon were fair game for reconstruction.

The most structurally significant sample is drawn from "The End," the climactic suite that closes *Abbey Road*. The drums from "Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (Reprise)" provide the rhythmic spine of "The Sounds of Science." Ringo Starr's drumming on that reprise is loose, almost martial, with a muscular snare that cuts through even the densest arrangements. When the Dust Brothers loop it and set it beneath layers of other material, it creates an uncanny collision: the most famous rock record ever made suddenly sounds like the bedrock of a hip-hop track. Your brain knows that snare from thousands of hours of passive listening, but your body is processing it as something entirely new.

"When I'm Sixty-Four" contributes multiple elements, and here the tonal irony is almost too rich to fully absorb. That song — McCartney's cheerful, music-hall meditation on domestic aging — is one of the warmest, most comfortable pieces in the Beatles' catalog. It evokes English parlors and birthday cakes and the quiet satisfaction of growing old gracefully. Chopped and repositioned inside "The Sounds of Science," its elements become unmoored from that comfort. They drift through the track like musical ghosts, familiar and strange at the same time.

The sound effects borrowed from "Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band" and from "Back in the U.S.S.R." add a layer of textural density that creates the impression of a live event perpetually about to begin, of music caught mid-preparation — a deeply strange feeling to sustain across an entire track.

But "The Sounds of Science" is not only a Beatles tribute. The Isaac Hayes sample — the hook and riff from "Walk From Regio's" — brings the track's funkiest and most earthbound element. Hayes was a master of slow-burn orchestration, and whatever element the Dust Brothers extracted from that record gives "The Sounds of Science" its center of gravity. The James Brown sample from "Get Up, Get Into It, Get Involved" brings a kinetic urgency, a sense of call-and-response and communal energy, that balances the more cerebral quality of the Beatles material.

The vocal samples push the track into even richer territory. Pato Banton's voice, pulled from "Don't Sniff Coke," introduces a reggae and dancehall inflection that widens the track's geographic and cultural reach. KRS-One, sampled from Boogie Down Productions' "My Philosophy," brings a sense of hip-hop intellectual rigor — a voice that in 1989 was deeply associated with rap's self-conscious exploration of its own cultural significance.

What makes "The Sounds of Science" ultimately so extraordinary is that it creates a unified aesthetic experience from wildly disparate materials. You are listening to The Beatles and James Brown and Isaac Hayes and reggae and hip-hop simultaneously, and yet the track has a single consistent emotional temperature — curious, playful, a little stoned, perpetually on the verge of revelation.

# CHECK YOUR HEAD (1992)

## "So What'cha Want"

If *Paul's Boutique* was the Beastie Boys as maximalists — every square inch of sonic space filled, every genre raided — then *Check Your Head* represented a deliberate and confident pullback toward something rawer and more immediate. "So What'cha Want" is the track that most elegantly straddles the line between the sample-driven approach that had made them famous and the live-band aesthetic they were developing. It is a stripped-down monster of a record — menacing, minimal, and irresistibly propulsive — and the way its samples function is almost the inverse of "The Sounds of Science." Where that track layers samples into baroque complexity, "So What'cha Want" uses them to create the illusion of pure, unmediated aggression.

The foundational sample is drawn from "I've Been Watching You" by the Southside Movement, a Chicago soul-funk outfit whose recordings have become something of a holy text for hip-hop producers. The elements drawn from this track form the rhythmic and harmonic backbone of "So What'cha Want," and what they contribute is a sense of organic, lived-in darkness that would be almost impossible to manufacture from scratch. This is music that sounds like it has been worn down to its essential components by repetition and use. When you hear that foundation beneath MCA, Ad-Rock, and Mike D's barked, sneering vocals, it sounds like the most natural thing in the world.

The Dick Hyman sound effect — lifted from "Give It Up or Turn It Loose" — illustrates how sophisticated the Beastie Boys' sampling sensibility had become by this point. Dick Hyman was a jazz pianist and organist whose career stretched from the 1950s into the 1980s, his recordings occupying a strange cultural space: expertly crafted, commercially savvy, and yet invisible to mass-market recognition. Pulling a sound effect from his catalog and deploying it in a track that would become one of the defining rock-adjacent hip-hop songs of the early 1990s is a classic Beastie Boys move — unexpected, slightly absurdist, and deeply informed.

The vocal sample from "Just Rhymin' With Biz," the classic Big Daddy Kane track featuring Biz Markie, brings the track into explicit dialogue with hip-hop's own history. By 1992, Kane and Biz Markie were figures from hip-hop's immediately preceding golden age — revered, influential, and already becoming the kinds of artists that younger rappers cited as touchstones. Sampling their voices is an act of tribute but also an act of contextualization: the Beastie Boys placing themselves within a lineage.

Perhaps the most conceptually interesting sample in "So What'cha Want" is the self-referential one: elements pulled from the Beastie Boys' own "Drunken Praying Mantis Style," a track from the *Polly Wog Stew* EP — the band's hardcore punk debut long before anyone thought of them as hip-hop artists. By sampling themselves from a period of their career that most of their audience would not recognize, the Beastie Boys are leaving coded messages about their own history within the architecture of a mainstream hit.

"So What'cha Want" does not announce its own sophistication. It does not invite you to admire the craft. It hits you in the chest and dares you to look away. Only on repeated, careful listening does the architecture reveal itself — the layers, the references, the deliberate choices that make a track this apparently simple feel so inexhaustibly full.

## "Pass the Mic"

"Pass the Mic" is, in several important ways, the most revealing track in the Beastie Boys' catalog — revealing not just about where the band had been, but about who they were in the process of becoming. For *Check Your Head*, they made a decision that seemed almost perverse in its counterintuitiveness: they picked up instruments. Real instruments. Guitars, bass, drums. They rehearsed. They played. And then they recorded an album that tried to hold hip-hop and live-band funk and punk rock in simultaneous suspension. "Pass the Mic" is the track that most fully captures the productive tension of that experiment.

The rhythmic foundation comes from two sources that initially seem almost contradictory in their implications. The drums from "I Walk on Guilded Splinters" by Johnny Jenkins — the Georgia guitarist whose 1970 album *Ton-Ton Macoute!* has become one of the most sampled records in the hip-hop canon — provide the track's propulsive, slightly off-kilter groove. Jenkins's record was a raw, psychedelic blues document, captured with Duane Allman and capturing something genuinely feral and uncontrolled. The drums on that recording carry all of that wildness: they feel like they might fall apart at any moment, which is precisely what makes them so compelling as a hip-hop foundation.

Set against that foundation, the bass from "Big Sur Suite" by Johnny Hammond — the jazz organist whose CTI Records output in the early 1970s has been mined extensively by hip-hop producers — brings a different kind of earthbound weight. Hammond's music operated at the intersection of soul-jazz and orchestrated funk, and the bass line extracted from "Big Sur Suite" carries all of that harmonic sophistication. Placed beneath the Jenkins drums, it creates a rhythmic platform that feels simultaneously ancient and completely contemporary.

The most sonically distinctive element of "Pass the Mic" is the flute hook drawn from "Choir" by James Newton. Newton is a classical flutist whose experimental work sits at the boundary between jazz improvisation and contemporary classical composition, and the fragment pulled from his recording transforms the entire character of the track. Where everything else is driving forward with aggression and momentum, the Newton flute introduces an element of airy spaciousness — almost meditative, almost devotional.

The Bad Brains elements — pulled from "Big Takeover," one of the defining tracks of American hardcore punk — represent the most autobiographically significant sample in "Pass the Mic." The Beastie Boys had come up in the New York hardcore scene before pivoting to hip-hop. They knew the Bad Brains not as a historical artifact but as contemporaries, as the band that, more than any other, had defined what punk could be in post-disco New York. Sampling the Bad Brains in 1992, at a moment when the Beastie Boys were consciously trying to reconnect with their punk roots by picking up instruments, is an act of both nostalgia and declaration: we come from here.

The EPMD vocal sample from "So Wat Cha Sayin'" and the Jimmie Walker vocal fragment from "The Black Prince Has Arrived" give the track its hip-hop lineage credentials. The Jimi Hendrix sound effect from "Tax Free" and the Southside Movement elements from "Funk Talk" complete the track's remarkable cross-genre architecture.

What "Pass the Mic" ultimately achieves is a demonstration that the shift to live instruments on *Check Your Head* was not a rejection of hip-hop identity but an expansion of it. By building a track that reaches from Bad Brains hardcore to CTI soul-jazz to James Newton experimental flute to EPMD boom-bap, the Beastie Boys proved that their ambitions had always been larger than any single genre could contain.

## "Jimmy James"

There is something almost cosmically audacious about "Jimmy James," the opening salvo from *Check Your Head*. To understand what the Beastie Boys were doing when they constructed this track, you have to first reckon with the sheer density of what they assembled: a song that is essentially a mosaic made entirely of borrowed genius, a hip-hop collage that pays tribute to one of rock music's most mythologized figures by dismantling his recorded legacy and rebuilding it in the image of New York street culture. The title itself is the first clue. Jimmy James was the performing name that James Marshall Hendrix used before he became Jimi Hendrix — before the Experience, before Woodstock, before the mythology calcified. By calling the song "Jimmy James," the Beastie Boys are reaching back to the raw, pre-canonical Hendrix: the sideman, the hungry guitarist playing clubs.

The Hendrix samples on "Jimmy James" are not deployed decoratively. They are the song's skeleton, nervous system, and skin simultaneously. The most immediately recognizable is the lift from "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)," perhaps Hendrix's most iconic studio performance. That opening wah-wah guitar figure — tectonic, prehistoric, almost confrontational in its confidence — serves as the track's central hook. When the Beastie Boys drop that riff into their arrangement, they are not just borrowing a sound. They are invoking an entire emotional universe: the late-1960s psychedelic blues tradition, the southern Black church filtered through distortion and feedback, the collision of Delta roots and Greenwich Village counterculture.

The sample from "Third Stone from the Sun," Hendrix's eerie, studio-manipulated instrumental from *Are You Experienced?*, adds a completely different textural register. Where "Voodoo Child" is volcanic and extroverted, "Third Stone" is alien, introverted, saturated with tape manipulation and reversed sounds. Its appearance in "Jimmy James" gestures toward Hendrix's more experimental, avant-garde tendencies. The "Are You Experienced?" sample reinforces this reading — that title question is almost philosophical in the context of the Beastie Boys' career trajectory. The "EXP" sample, the brief spoken-word intro to *Axis: Bold as Love*, adds yet another layer of conceptual texture: Hendrix as satirist and concept artist.

"Still Raining, Still Dreaming," a sprawling instrumental blues from the posthumous *South Saturn Delta*, brings in Hendrix's most raw, unfinished studio work — music that was never intended as a final statement. The Jimi Hendrix/Curtis Knight version of "Happy Birthday" adds biographical specificity. Curtis Knight was one of the bandleaders Hendrix worked for during his pre-Experience years as a sideman — the very period evoked by the title "Jimmy James."

But "Jimmy James" is not only a Hendrix tribute. The sample from The Turtles' "I'm Chief Kamanawanalea" introduces a completely different emotional register — sunny, melodic, almost comically breezy West Coast pop. The juxtaposition is the point: the Beastie Boys have always operated through the collision of cultural registers, and pulling a Turtles sample into a Hendrix tribute could only make sense in the hip-hop tradition of radical collage.

"Beat Bop" by Rammellzee and K-Rob, produced by Jean-Michel Basquiat in 1983, is one of hip-hop's most legendary and obscure recordings — a nine-minute spoken-word meditation on urban life and artistic identity pressed in tiny quantities that became an immediate collector's rarity. The Fearless Four's "Rockin' It" and Mantronix's "Fresh Is the Word" connect the track back to the machine-age origins of hip-hop production, contrasting powerfully with Hendrix's deeply human, blues-rooted guitar work.

What "Jimmy James" ultimately argues — through its sampling choices more than through anything its MCs explicitly say — is that American vernacular music in the late twentieth century is a single, interconnected tradition. Hendrix and Rammellzee and The Turtles and Mantronix all live in the same ecosystem, all draw from the same well of cultural energy.

## "The Maestro"

If "Jimmy James" is a cathedral, "The Maestro" is a corner store — smaller in scale, more intimate in its atmosphere, but in many ways more revealing about how the Beastie Boys thought about the relationship between musical genres, cultural traditions, and the possibilities of hip-hop as a synthesizing art form. Built around four vocal samples from sources that have almost nothing obvious in common, "The Maestro" achieves something quietly remarkable: it creates a sense of community across cultural lines through the sheer act of juxtaposition.

The sample from Bad Brains' "Supertouch/Shitfit" is the foundation that gives the track its underlying tension and energy. Bad Brains were one of the most singular bands in American music history: a Black hardcore punk group from Washington, D.C. who played faster, harder, and more technically accomplished than virtually anyone in the punk and hardcore scenes of the early 1980s, while simultaneously being among the most accomplished reggae musicians in that same city's tradition. "Supertouch/Shitfit" is from the hardcore side of that equation — aggressive, kinetic, almost violently forward-moving — and its vocal elements introduce a charge of physical energy that keeps "The Maestro" from settling into any kind of comfortable groove.

The Beastie Boys' affinity for Bad Brains runs deep and is biographical rather than merely aesthetic. As young people growing up in New York City in the early 1980s, they came of age in a moment when hardcore punk and hip-hop were not yet the separate cultural worlds they would later become. Sampling "Supertouch/Shitfit" on "The Maestro" is thus partly an act of tribute and partly an act of autobiography: this is where we come from.

The Cheech and Chong sample from "Don't Bug Me" shifts the register completely. Cheech and Chong were the preeminent comedy duo of the 1970s counterculture, their routines saturated with marijuana humor and a particular brand of laid-back Los Angeles irreverence. Their appearance in "The Maestro" introduces an element of pure absurdism — a refusal to take the track too seriously, a reminder that pleasure and laughter are legitimate artistic values. The Beastie Boys have always had a complicated relationship with comedy, and the Cheech and Chong sample here is a small but precise restatement of that position.

Lee "Scratch" Perry's "Kojak" brings the track into an entirely different sonic and spiritual universe. Perry is one of the supreme geniuses of recorded music in the twentieth century — the Jamaican producer who developed a production aesthetic so idiosyncratic and visionary that it essentially invented dub reggae as a genre. His work is simultaneously deeply rooted in Jamaican folk tradition, Rastafarian spirituality, and a kind of psychedelic experimentalism. "Kojak" is a characteristically eccentric Perry production — warm, elastic, permeated with his unmistakable vocal personality. Perry's influence on how producers think about rhythm, space, and the manipulation of recorded sound extends far beyond reggae into virtually every genre that has taken the studio seriously as a creative environment.

The Eleventh Hour's "Medley: Sock It to Me/It's Your Thing" closes out the track's vocal sample palette with a piece of funk-adjacent soul from the broad tradition of late-1960s and early-1970s Black popular music. "It's Your Thing" is an Isley Brothers song that became one of the defining funk statements of the era, a declaration of individual autonomy and self-determination set to an irresistible groove.

Taken together, the four samples on "The Maestro" constitute a map of the Beastie Boys' cultural world: hardcore punk, countercultural comedy, Jamaican dub, Black funk and soul. What is remarkable is that these traditions actually do connect at the level of history and influence. "The Maestro" is the Beastie Boys making a claim about themselves — quietly, without fanfare, through the eloquence of their crate-digging choices. A maestro commands a wide range of instruments and voices and can hear the unity beneath apparent diversity.

## "Stand Together"

"Stand Together" presents a different kind of analytical challenge because the samples it employs exist in a fundamentally different relationship to the finished product. Where "Jimmy James" is essentially built from samples and "The Maestro" uses vocal lifts to populate a sonic environment, "Stand Together" deploys its samples in dialogue with live performance — and that dialogue is where the song's deepest meanings are generated.

The central sample, and the one that most immediately shapes the song's character, is drawn from "Slivadiv" by Back Door, a British jazz-rock fusion trio who were active in the early 1970s and who occupied the fertile space between jazz improvisation, progressive rock, and exploratory instrumental music. Back Door were a critically appreciated cult act whose music was built around interplay between bass, saxophone, and drums. "Slivadiv" is a characteristically sophisticated piece: rhythmically complex, harmonically adventurous, the kind of track that reveals new details on each encounter. The riff that the Beastie Boys lift from this track provides "Stand Together" with its melodic and harmonic identity — a jazz-inflected figure that gives the song its distinctive atmosphere of seriousness and cool.

The choice of Back Door as a source demonstrates the depth of the Beastie Boys' record collecting, the willingness to dig past the obvious into the overlooked and the obscure. The jazz inflection of the sample connects "Stand Together" to a tradition that runs through hip-hop's DNA in complex and not always visible ways, and it suggests that their turn toward live instrumentation was not a departure from hip-hop but an extension of one of the music's core impulses.

The drums on "Stand Together" come from a lift from "Kissing My Love" by Cold Blood, a Bay Area soul-funk band whose vocalist Lydia Pense was one of the great blue-eyed soul singers of the era. What is notable is how seamlessly it integrates with the live drumming that Mike Diamond also contributes to the track. The sample and the live performance exist in a relationship of mutual reinforcement: the sample provides historical grounding; the live playing provides freshness and spontaneity.

The bass sample from Grady Tate's "Be Black Baby" introduces another dimension. Grady Tate was primarily known as a jazz drummer and vocalist who worked with everyone from Wes Montgomery to Quincy Jones. The bass line the Beastie Boys lift from this track is deep, warm, and absolutely authoritative. Bass in the African-American musical tradition carries enormous weight, both physically and culturally; it is the instrument most directly connected to the body, to dance, to the physical experience of music as a communal act.

The James Brown samples — from "The Payback," Brown's ferociously intense 1973 funk masterwork — bring the track's most powerful and direct reference. Placing Brown's influence in dialogue with Back Door's jazz-rock, Cold Blood's soul, and Grady Tate's funk-jazz creates a richer, more complicated genealogy than simple tribute would allow.

What "Stand Together" ultimately demonstrates is that the Beastie Boys' live-band aesthetic was never a rejection of hip-hop's sampling tradition, but a fusion of the two approaches into something new. "Stand Together," with its layered archaeology of samples and its commitment to real-time musical performance, is as complete a statement of that identity as anything on *Check Your Head*.

---

## "Professor Booty"

*Check Your Head* arrived in 1992 as a kind of manifesto. "Professor Booty" is one of the album's most dizzying constructions — a track that sounds simultaneously like a block party, a late-night jazz club, a comedy record, and a hip-hop laboratory. To understand why it feels that way, you have to excavate the ten distinct samples buried inside it, each one a geological layer from a different corner of American popular music history.

Start with the track's most conceptually resonant sample: the material drawn from *Wild Style*, the 1983 film widely regarded as the first genuine document of hip-hop culture on celluloid. The "Connie's Store" segment represents more than just a musical lift — it's the Beastie Boys reaching back to touch the foundational mythology of the genre they're working in. *Wild Style* captured New York's South Bronx scene at the precise moment hip-hop was cohering as a unified cultural force, weaving together graffiti, breakdancing, DJing, and MCing. By sampling it in 1992, the Beasties are performing an act of historical self-positioning, acknowledging the tradition they inherited while simultaneously scrambling its DNA into something new.

The percussion architecture is built from three distinct drum sources. The drums from Jimmy Smith's "I'm Gonna Love You Just a Little More Babe" bring something essentially soulful and slightly languid to the mix. Smith, primarily known as a Hammond B-3 organ virtuoso who helped define the soul-jazz sound of the 1960s, recorded with a pocket and looseness that characterized Blue Note Records at its most swinging. Kool & the Gang's "Give It Up" contributes a tighter, more processed drum sound oriented toward the dancefloor. Freda Payne's "The Easiest Way to Fall" delivers a third drum layer. Layering these three drum sources creates a rhythmic conversation across decades.

The bass line anchored to Freddie Hubbard's "Uncle Albert/Admiral Halsey" is one of those beautifully obscure sample choices that reveals how deep the Beastie Boys and their producer Mario Caldato Jr. were digging. Hubbard was a titan of hard bop trumpet, a Blue Note Records artist whose work with Art Blakey's Jazz Messengers defined the forward edge of 1960s jazz. Jazz bass lines — with their melodic intelligence and rhythmic flexibility — give the track a kind of walking, searching quality.

The Buddy Miles Express material from "Let Your Lovelight Shine" injects the raw, slightly overdriven energy of a musician who had played in Jimi Hendrix's Band of Gypsys. Willie Henderson's "Loose Booty" contributes vocals, and conceptually the song's title carries Henderson's fingerprints. Freddie Prinze's "Hunga Rican" introduces genuine surrealism — Prinze was a comedian and actor who died tragically in 1977 at age 22, and sampling his comedy records introduces a layer of cultural and historical pathos. Schoolly D's "Gucci Time" contribution brings street-level hip-hop authority.

Richard Evans' "Pie Daddy's Dream" provides some of the track's more sophisticated harmonic color, while the Wild Style material ties it all back to hip-hop's own origin story. What "Professor Booty" achieves through all this layering is a genuine act of musical archaeology made to feel effortless. The ten sources span from Blue Note jazz to Chicago funk, from Hollywood comedy records to the foundational documents of hip-hop culture. The genius of the construction is that none of it sounds like homework — it sounds like a party where everyone arrived from a different decade and found a way to dance to the same beat.

---

## "Finger Lickin' Good"

If "Professor Booty" is the sound of a music library being ransacked with joyful abandon, then "Finger Lickin' Good" is something more prismatic and strange — a track that reaches across genre borders with deliberate, almost theoretical ambition. The seven samples embedded in this track span folk, psychedelic soul, jazz, funk, and early disco, and the way they're assembled creates something that genuinely doesn't sound like any of its component parts.

The most audacious element is the vocal sample from Bob Dylan's "Just Like Tom Thumb's Blues." That song, released on *Highway 61 Revisited* in 1965, is already one of the stranger entries in Dylan's catalog — a vivid, surrealist dispatch from somewhere on the Mexico border, full of images that feel like they've been lifted from a fever dream. Sampling those vocals and transplanting them into a 1992 hip-hop context is an act of genuinely radical genre transgression. By 1992, hip-hop had already developed its own canonical sources, its preferred crate-digging territories (James Brown, George Clinton, soul, funk, jazz), but Bob Dylan? That's a different kind of move entirely. It signals that the Beastie Boys are willing to reach into the white rock and folk canon with the same magpie curiosity they apply to Black American music — not to appropriate but to integrate, to treat all American popular music as a shared commons.

The 5th Dimension's "Aquarius/Let the Sunshine In" carries the weight of a very precise cultural moment — the dizzy optimism of the late psychedelic era, the Age of Aquarius fantasy before the 1970s arrived to complicate it. Sampling "multiple elements" from it in a Beastie Boys track introduces a layer of irony without necessarily being ironic — the psychedelic innocence of the source collides with the knowing sophistication of the sample user.

The hook and riff borrowed from Johnny Hammond's "Breakout" serves as one of the structural anchors of the track, giving it harmonic identity that the more fragmentary vocal samples can orbit around. Hammond was a jazz organist who gravitated increasingly toward jazz-funk crossover in the 1970s, working with sophisticated arrangements that maintained the raw organ energy that was his signature.

Rahsaan Roland Kirk's contribution from "Freaks for the Festival" introduces genuine avant-garde jazz. Kirk was one of the most extraordinary and eccentric musicians in jazz history — capable of playing multiple horns simultaneously, a tireless innovator who bridged hard bop, free jazz, and R&B. He was also a fierce advocate for what he called "black classical music" — his presence gives "Finger Lickin' Good" an air of jazz intellectualism.

Cerrone's "Rocket in the Pocket (Live)" brings a European disco influence. Marc Cerrone was a French drummer and producer who created some of the most sophisticated electronic and orchestral disco records of the late 1970s — works like *Supernature* and *Love in C Minor* that have since been reassessed as genuine artistic achievements.

"Maceo" from Maceo and All the King's Men is a reference deep in the James Brown extended universe. Maceo Parker, the alto saxophonist who was indispensable to James Brown's sound from the mid-1960s onward, recorded this as part of a collection of tracks with other Brown alumni. Sampling vocals and lyrics from this source acknowledges hip-hop's fundamental debt to the James Brown tradition while going one layer deeper — not sampling Brown himself but sampling the people who made Brown sound like Brown.

Finally, Sly & the Family Stone's "Are You Ready" provides the drum foundation, connecting the track to one of the most sampled artists in hip-hop history. The result is a track that functions as a genuinely psychedelic experience in the original sense — mind-expanding and full of unexpected juxtapositions.

## "Funky Boss"

"Funky Boss" is the most conceptually coherent and musically adventurous track in this trio, and in many ways the key to understanding what *Check Your Head* was attempting as a cultural project. The seven samples embedded in "Funky Boss" create a sonic map of the Black Atlantic — a term the cultural theorist Paul Gilroy would use to describe the circulation of music, people, and ideas across the African diaspora.

The Ohio Players' "Funky Worm" provides the vocal and lyrical material that gives "Funky Boss" its immediate funk credentials. The Ohio Players were one of the defining acts of early 1970s funk, creating music that was simultaneously raw and sophisticated, carnally direct and musically inventive. "Funky Worm" from 1973 features one of the most recognizable synthesizer sounds in the entire funk canon.

The presence of not one but three Jamaican reggae sources in "Funky Boss" is what makes the track genuinely remarkable. Barrington Levy's "Under Mi Sensi," Big Youth's "Solomon a Gunday," and The Wailers' "Duppy Conqueror" represent three distinct generations and stylistic registers within Jamaican music, and their combined presence reveals a deep engagement with reggae and dub that goes beyond simple sampling opportunism.

Barrington Levy occupies a particular place in reggae history as a figure who bridged the roots and dancehall eras. "Under Mi Sensi" is classic dancehall — assertive, rhythmically dynamic, built around the driving riddim that defined Jamaican music in the early 1980s as it began to incorporate digital production.

Big Youth represents the tradition of the DJ or toaster, the Jamaican vocal performer who chants and declaims over instrumental reggae tracks — a practice that is one of the most direct antecedents of hip-hop MCing. By sampling Big Youth's "Solomon a Gunday," the Beastie Boys are reaching back to touch one of hip-hop's own origin points.

The Wailers' "Duppy Conqueror" is the most historically loaded sample in "Funky Boss." This track, from the 1970 album *Soul Rebels*, comes from the period just before the Wailers' international breakthrough, when they were still recording for producer Lee "Scratch" Perry and producing some of the most psychedelically deep roots reggae ever captured on tape. The title is significant — a "duppy" in Jamaican folk belief is a ghost or malevolent spirit, and a "duppy conqueror" is someone who has mastered the spirit world.

The Upsetters' "Return of Django" provides multiple elements that bring the dub dimension explicitly into play. Lee "Scratch" Perry's production work with The Upsetters essentially invented dub reggae, and his production aesthetic — heavy bass, reverb-drenched percussion, strategic use of space and silence — directly influenced the development of hip-hop production in New York, where Jamaican producers and DJs were essential early figures.

Thin Lizzy's "Showdown" introduces the one non-Black, non-American element into the sample palette. Thin Lizzy, led by Phil Lynott — himself of Afro-Irish heritage — occupied a unique position in 1970s rock, technically working in hard rock territory but shaped by Lynott's complex relationship to Blackness and American funk and soul.

Richard Pryor's "Acid" completes the picture by bringing in stand-up comedy as spoken word material. Pryor was among the most verbally and rhythmically sophisticated American comedians ever recorded, and sampling his words reveals the structural kinship between stand-up performance and MCing — both traditions that depend on precise rhythmic speech and the ability to hold an audience through verbal authority alone.

"Funky Boss" ultimately demonstrates that the Beastie Boys understood *Check Your Head* as a meditation on the diasporic imagination — on the way music travels, transforms, and generates new forms as it crosses geographic and cultural boundaries.

# ILL COMMUNICATION (1994)

---

## "Sure Shot"

To understand "Sure Shot," you first have to understand what it means to dig through crates in the early 1990s. The Beastie Boys — Adam Horovitz (Ad-Rock), Michael Diamond (Mike D), and Adam Yauch (MCA) — had, by 1994, fully shed any remaining association with the frat-punk chaos of *Licensed to Ill* and were operating as genuine scholars of sound. *Ill Communication* was their fourth studio album and represented a peak of the crate-digging ethos that had come to define the golden age of hip-hop sampling.

The spine of "Sure Shot" is built from a loop lifted from "Howlin' for Judy," a track by flautist Jeremy Steig from his 1969 album *Legwork*. Steig was not a household name even among jazz enthusiasts — he was a session and ensemble player, a working musician of the post-bop era who operated on the fringes of the avant-garde. "Howlin' for Judy" is a piece of hard-driving, exploratory jazz flute work, the kind of music that existed in a very specific cultural and commercial niche in 1969, pressed on vinyl, circulated among jazz collectors, and largely forgotten by the mass market long before hip-hop came along to resurrect it.

What the Beastie Boys heard in that recording was a melodic hook so intrinsically compelling that it could anchor an entire hip-hop track. The flute riff that opens "Sure Shot" is bright, slightly piercing, and rhythmically asymmetrical in a way that immediately catches the ear. In the context of 1994, when the dominant sonic palette of hip-hop was drifting toward the warmer, bassier textures of G-funk on the West Coast and the raw, concrete grit of New York hardcore, the decision to foreground a jazz flute from a 1969 record was genuinely countercultural.

This choice situates "Sure Shot" within a broader movement happening across hip-hop in the early 1990s, sometimes called the jazz-rap or Native Tongues aesthetic, in which artists deliberately reached back to the jazz tradition as a way of asserting cultural legitimacy and musical sophistication. Groups like A Tribe Called Quest, De La Soul, Gang Starr, and Digable Planets were all mining similar territory. But the Beastie Boys' use of the Steig sample is distinctive because of its sheer boldness — the flute is not buried in the mix or treated as a subtle texture. It is the track.

There is also something specifically transgressive about centering a hip-hop record on a flute. The instrument carries connotations of classical music and a certain kind of European refinement. In hip-hop, an instrument associated with softness gets recontextualized as something hard and funky. The loop is chopped and placed over a drum pattern that gives it a driving, insistent urgency. The Beastie Boys were always interested in the collision of cultural registers.

The second most important sample comes from a completely different register. The track incorporates audio from "The Funny Sides of Moms Mabley (Side 1)," a comedy album recorded by Jackie "Moms" Mabley, one of the most important stand-up comedians of the twentieth century. Mabley was a Black woman who began performing in the 1920s, rose to fame in the chitlin circuit, recorded a series of enormously successful comedy albums in the 1960s, and became one of the first Black female comedians to achieve mainstream television exposure. The decision to sample Mabley brings the voice of an elderly Black woman from mid-century America into a mid-1990s hip-hop track, a generational bridge that is both respectful and irreverent. Mabley's voice carries the grain of an earlier century — there is an analog warmth to it, a sense of time and history embedded in the very texture of the recording.

The Run-DMC elements sampled from "Rock the House" are a gesture of hip-hop genealogy — Run-DMC being one of the foundational groups without whom the Beastie Boys' own career would have been impossible. The "Change the Beat (Female Version)" by Beside contributes one of hip-hop's most sampled vocal elements, a piece of recorded sound so deeply embedded in the DNA of the genre that its presence functions almost like a citation. The Little Caesars Pizza commercial sound effect is pure Beastie Boys absurdism — the injection of commercial detritus into art.

Together, these elements make "Sure Shot" something close to a manifesto of the Beastie Boys' artistic philosophy: jazz sophistication, hip-hop genealogy, comedy, cultural memory, and absurdist humor all woven into a single track.

---

## "Get It Together"

"Get It Together" is one of the richest texts in the Beastie Boys' catalog, a track that rewards close listening precisely because its sampling strategy is so multilayered and so conscious of the cultural conversations it is entering and amplifying. It features a guest verse from Q-Tip of A Tribe Called Quest — a collaboration that is not incidental but rather the organizing principle around which the track's entire sonic philosophy is arranged.

By 1994, Q-Tip was one of the most celebrated voices in hip-hop, the central MC and primary producer behind A Tribe Called Quest's string of landmark albums. Q-Tip's aesthetic was defined by a commitment to jazz-influenced sampling, melodic sophistication, and a conscious rejection of gangster rap's dominant narrative formulas. He was a key figure in the Native Tongues collective, a loose affiliation of groups united by shared values around Afrocentric positivity and musical eclecticism. The Beastie Boys, by 1993 and 1994, were orbiting this same cultural space, and the collaboration on "Get It Together" is therefore not just a celebrity guest feature — it is a statement of aesthetic solidarity.

The vocal and lyrical elements sampled from "Headless Heroes" by Eugene McDaniels represent one of the most politically charged sample sources on the album. McDaniels released *Headless Heroes of the Apocalypse* in 1971, an album so politically radical and directly critical of American imperialism and racial capitalism that it reportedly alarmed figures in the Nixon administration and contributed to Atlantic Records withdrawing promotional support for the record. To sample Eugene McDaniels in 1994 is to reach back to a specific moment of Black radical consciousness in American music.

The "Aquarius/Let the Sunshine In" sample sourced from The Moog Machine's 1969 record — a synthesizer version of the Hair medley — has a lush, slightly clinical quality that contrasts interestingly with the organic warmth of the other elements. Fred Wesley and the Horny Horns' drum contribution from "Four Play" brings a different energy entirely. Fred Wesley was James Brown's trombonist and arranger for years, a central figure in the development of funk as a musical language. The drums lifted from this recording have a particular snap and authority that comes from the discipline of live funk performance.

The James Brown "Escape-Ism" vocal sample is both musically functional and symbolically enormous. Placing Brown's voice in "Get It Together" — the Godfather in the room — adds the weight of the tradition from which all of this music descends. Biz Markie's appearance, sampled from "A One Two," brings the "Clown Prince of Rap" into the mix as a gesture of hip-hop affection and historical connection.

Spoonie Gee, one of the earliest MCs in hip-hop history, is sampled from "Spoonie Is Back" in ways that contribute multiple elements. Spoonie Gee's music comes from the very origins of recorded hip-hop, from the era when the genre was still finding its form. Perhaps the most unexpected element is the vocal contribution from Leroy "Horsemouth" Wallace's "Herb Vendor," a reggae track that introduces a distinctly Caribbean flavor and speaks to hip-hop's broader genealogical relationship with Jamaican sound system culture — a connection that runs from the genre's very beginnings in the South Bronx.

---

## "Flute Loop"

If "Sure Shot" is the polished, commercially accessible face of *Ill Communication*'s jazz-inflected crate-digging, then "Flute Loop" is its more experimental, esoteric twin — a track that digs deeper into the archive, reaches further into the margins of American and global music history, and constructs something genuinely strange and compelling from the raw materials it excavates.

The foundation of "Flute Loop" is built on elements sampled from "Flute Thing" by The Blues Project, a 1966 recording that stands as a remarkable artifact of the moment when American rock and jazz were most productively in conversation with each other. The Blues Project was a New York City band that formed in the mid-1960s and occupied a fascinating position in the musical landscape of that era — blues-rooted, folk-influenced, psychedelically inclined, and genuinely jazz-curious. "Flute Thing" was one of their signature pieces, built around a prominent flute melody that was unusual for a rock context and gave the track an airy, modal quality that anticipated some of the psychedelic explorations that would follow later in the decade.

The fact that "Flute Loop" is built on The Blues Project while "Sure Shot" is built on Jeremy Steig creates an interesting internal dialogue within *Ill Communication*. Both tracks foreground the flute as the primary melodic instrument, but through very different source materials — one drawn from the world of avant-garde jazz, the other from the rock-jazz crossover of 1960s New York. Together, they suggest a preoccupation with this particular instrument and its capacity to create a specific kind of melodic affect.

The vocal and lyrical elements sampled from Lee "Scratch" Perry's "Dub Revolution" introduce one of the most extraordinary figures in twentieth-century music. Lee Perry was arguably the most creative and visionary producer in the history of reggae music, the architect of dub as a distinct musical form. Perry's own solo work operated at the absolute outer edge of what recording technology was capable of producing, often in his legendary Black Ark studio in Kingston. Sampling Lee Perry is not a casual decision. His voice is singular — heavily accented, rhythmically unpredictable, often cryptic or surrealist in its content. Dub music is itself a form of sampling practice, in which existing recordings are deconstructed and rebuilt into new arrangements. The Beastie Boys sampling Perry for a track called "Flute Loop" — a title that openly declares its own constructed, repetitive nature — is an act of genre-level rhyming.

The drum elements in "Flute Loop" come from two distinct sources. Lonnie Smith — the organist Dr. Lonnie Smith, a Blue Note Records artist — contributes drum sounds from "Spinning Wheel" that have a particular heaviness and deliberateness. Young-Holt Unlimited, the Chicago-based trio led by bassist Eldee Young and drummer Isaac "Red" Holt, contribute drum elements from "Rubber Lips." Holt was one of the most propulsive and inventive drummers of his generation, and when his work is looped, it creates exactly the kind of forward momentum that hip-hop production requires.

The spoken word sample from Richard Pryor's "I Spy Cops" is, alongside the Moms Mabley sample in "Sure Shot," one of the most significant comedy deployments across *Ill Communication*. Pryor's "I Spy Cops" routine combines the visceral personal testimony of someone who has experienced police surveillance with his razor-sharp observational precision. The presence of this sample in "Flute Loop" introduces a note of social critique and racial politics that grounds the track's more abstract musical explorations in concrete American reality. By 1994, police brutality and the relationship between Black communities and law enforcement were central issues in American public life — the Rodney King beating and the subsequent L.A. uprising were just two years in the past.

The vocal elements from The Micronawts' "Smurph Across the Surf" represent one of the more obscure sample sources on an album already characterized by its commitment to obscurity. Their presence contributes a quality of playfulness to "Flute Loop" that prevents the track from becoming too earnest or heavy, despite the political gravity introduced by the Pryor sample. "Flute Loop" as a whole demonstrates what the Beastie Boys were capable of when they abandoned commercial imperatives entirely and simply followed the music wherever it led.

---

## "Sabotage"

There are songs that define a moment, and then there are songs that define an era — songs so fully realized, so ferociously alive, that they seem to arrive not from a recording studio but from some place outside of time. "Sabotage," the lead single from *Ill Communication*, is unambiguously the latter. It is a song that sounds like a controlled explosion, like the moment before a car chase turns catastrophic, like punk rock and hip-hop crashing into each other at full speed and somehow producing something more vital than either could manage alone. And what makes the achievement all the more remarkable is how it was built: not from the mosaic of samples that had defined so much of the Beastie Boys' earlier work, but from the raw, unprocessed energy of three musicians playing in a room together.

The track is built on a live rhythm section that hits with the immediacy of early hardcore punk. Mike D's drumming is the foundation: a massive, thunderous groove that carries the kind of physical weight you feel in your chest before you consciously hear it. The drums don't swing — they bludgeon, hammering out a tempo that feels like barely contained chaos. Over this, Horovitz's guitar work is a wall of distorted menace — blunt and purposeful, the sonic equivalent of a fist. The bass — driven by Yauch — locks in beneath everything with a low-end insistence that gives the track its relentless momentum. Collectively, the band sounds like they are playing for their lives.

Rick Rubin's fingerprints are all over the production. Rubin, who had worked with the Beastie Boys on *Licensed to Ill*, had by 1994 become the preeminent producer of heavy, guitar-driven rock with a stripped-down aesthetic. His production philosophy centers on removal: stripping away the unnecessary until what remains is inescapable. The song does not sound compressed or overproduced. It sounds enormous and immediate, like a live performance captured in amber at the moment of maximum intensity.

Then there is the Spike Jonze music video, without which any honest assessment of "Sabotage" would be incomplete. Jonze, who would go on to direct *Being John Malkovich* and *Her*, was already establishing himself as one of the most visually inventive directors working in the music video format. His concept — shooting the Beastie Boys as a fictional 1970s cop show called "Alaskan Pipeline," replete with cheap polyester suits, catastrophically large mustaches, car chases, and absurdist villain confrontations — was a masterstroke of deadpan genre parody. The video looks authentically cheap in exactly the right way, channeling *Starsky and Hutch* and *The Streets of San Francisco* with an affection that tips over into genuine homage.

Perhaps the most remarkable chapter in "Sabotage"'s story is what happened after its release: the song became one of the most sampled tracks in its genre, its DNA distributed across hip-hop, electronic music, and even jazz. Atmosphere's "Puppets" is among the most artistically successful interpolations, with Slug deploying the track's raw energy beneath introspective, narrative-driven lyricism. The 1.8.7 electronic production "Sabotage" finds the track's rhythmic bones transferable to dance music contexts. Quelle Chris's "Beastie Q" uses elements of the song in a meta-referential framework that comments on the Beastie Boys' legacy itself.

Most intriguing of all may be Hedvig Mollestad Trio's "Beastie, Beastie," which transplants the compositional DNA of "Sabotage" into an acoustic jazz context. Strip away the distortion and the tempo and the cultural context, and "Sabotage" is a composition with genuine melodic and rhythmic content, something that can survive abstraction and re-contextualization. The fact that it works as jazz improvisation source material tells us that the Beastie Boys were not simply making noise in 1994; they were making music in the deepest sense of the word.

## "B-Boys Makin' With the Freak Freak"

While "Sabotage" announced itself with the force of a detonation, *Ill Communication* had a quieter, more cerebral dimension. "B-Boys Makin' With the Freak Freak" is a rich, jazz-inflected excavation of Black American musical history that demonstrates the group's extraordinary skill as crate diggers, cultural historians, and sonic architects.

The foundational atmosphere owes an enormous debt to Mantan Moreland, the African American comedian and actor whose career stretched from the 1930s through the early 1970s. Moreland was a complex figure in American entertainment history — celebrated for his physical comedy and improvisational genius, but also freighted with the compromised legacy of a Black performer operating within the constraints of Hollywood's most racist era. His vocal sample introduces a quality of vintage Americana that the Beastie Boys were increasingly drawn to during the *Check Your Head* and *Ill Communication* period. Moreland's voice carries the grain of an earlier century. By incorporating this voice into a mid-nineties hip-hop production, the Beastie Boys are creating a kind of audio time travel, insisting that the continuum of Black American performance runs unbroken from Moreland's vaudeville-adjacent comedy through jazz through hip-hop.

The Charles Rouse sample is where the track's musical backbone comes from. Rouse was a tenor saxophonist who spent a crucial decade as a member of Thelonious Monk's quartet, recording some of the most celebrated small-group jazz of the early 1960s. "Hopscotch" is characteristic Rouse — melodically angular, rhythmically sophisticated, with a warm, slightly grainy tone. The Beastie Boys deploy multiple elements from this track, working with the material as active composers: extracting melodic fragments, rhythmic motifs, and textural elements and reorganizing them into a new architecture.

The inclusion of "Beat Bop" by Rammellzee and K-Rob represents one of the track's most historically resonant choices. "Beat Bop" is a genuine artifact of hip-hop's early avant-garde — produced by Jean-Michel Basquiat in 1983, it is a twelve-inch single that sounds like nothing else in the rap catalog of its era. Rammellzee, the artist and theorist who developed the elaborate philosophical framework he called "Ikonoklast Panzerism," was one of hip-hop's most genuinely eccentric figures. By sampling this record, the Beastie Boys are drawing a direct line from hip-hop's early experimental edge to their own mid-nineties explorations.

The Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five sample, drawn from "Flash It to the Beat," completes the track's survey of hip-hop's foundational era. Flash's technical innovations — he essentially invented turntablism as a systematic practice — cannot be overstated. The Furious Five were among the earliest MCs to demonstrate that rap lyrics could carry real narrative weight and social commentary. The Beastie Boys' decision to incorporate multiple elements from this track is both an homage and an argument: they are positioning themselves within a lineage, claiming kinship with the foundational figures of the art form they have made their own.

What emerges from all of these source materials is a track that feels like a conversation across time — different voices from different moments in Black American music history brought together in a space of shared inquiry. The best sample-based music does not erase its sources but illuminates them. "B-Boys Makin' With the Freak Freak" is as much an act of curation and education as it is an act of musical creation.

# HELLO NASTY (1998)

---

## "Body Movin'"

By 1998, the Beastie Boys had been making records for the better part of two decades, and *Hello Nasty* found them operating at a peak of creative confidence. "Body Movin'," the album's most irresistibly kinetic track, represents the sweet spot between all of their influences — a song that feels simultaneously like a vintage party record, a sophisticated piece of sample archaeology, and a fully contemporary production achievement.

The centerpiece of the track's sample construction is its most geographically surprising element: material from Amral's Trinidad Cavaliers' recording of "Oye Como Va." This is a song with a complex provenance — composed by Tito Puente in 1963, it is one of the definitive works of Latin jazz, later made internationally famous by Santana's 1970 rock adaptation on *Abraxas*. The Trinidad Cavaliers were a Trinidadian steel pan orchestra, and their version of "Oye Como Va" exists at a fascinating crossroads: an Afro-Cuban jazz composition played on instruments developed in the Caribbean, produced in a musical tradition with its own distinct rhythmic and tonal characteristics. The steel pan version carries a lightness and shimmer that distinguishes it from both Puente's original and Santana's guitar-driven adaptation — the metallic resonance of the pans gives the melody a quality that is simultaneously warm and crystalline, festive and slightly otherworldly.

There is something philosophically rich about this chain of cultural transmission: an Afro-Cuban melody composed by a Puerto Rican bandleader, recorded by a Trinidadian steel pan orchestra, sampled by a group of hip-hop artists from New York, creating a party anthem for a global audience. "Body Movin'" is, in this sense, a document of music's extraordinary capacity to travel across cultural boundaries, accumulating new meanings at each stage without losing the core vitality that made it worth transmitting.

The drum architecture draws from two sources that could not, on the surface, seem more different: McDonald and Giles' "Tomorrow's People - the Children of Today" and Graham Central Station's "The Jam." McDonald and Giles were a British progressive rock duo — Ian McDonald, the multi-instrumentalist who co-founded King Crimson, and Michael Giles, Crimson's original drummer — who recorded a single, ambitious album in 1970. Giles' background was in jazz and his approach to rhythm was compositional rather than merely supportive, creating patterns of genuine complexity. Graham Central Station's "The Jam," on the other hand, comes from the hard funk of Larry Graham's band — music that is supremely physical, designed to make bodies move. By combining drum elements from these two sources, the Beastie Boys create a rhythmic hybrid that captures the complexity of the former and the danceability of the latter.

The spoken word sample from Ed Durlacher's "Commands and Music" adds another layer of temporal and cultural texture. Durlacher was an American square dance caller and folk music enthusiast who recorded instructional records in the 1940s and 1950s, his voice carrying the hearty, commanding quality of someone accustomed to directing groups of people through physical movement. There is a meta-textual appropriateness to deploying a dance caller's voice in a track titled "Body Movin'."

Perhaps the most self-reflexive element is the incorporation of a sample from the Beastie Boys' own "Slow and Low," a track from *Licensed to Ill* (1986) that was actually written by Run-D.M.C. By sampling their own earlier work, the group is creating a self-referential loop — acknowledging their own history and the way that a long career inevitably involves a dialogue with one's own earlier work.

"Body Movin'" became one of the signature tracks of *Hello Nasty*, which debuted at number one in both the United States and the United Kingdom. The track's success demonstrated that the Beastie Boys' approach to sample-based music — reaching across genre lines and temporal boundaries — could produce something with genuine popular appeal while retaining the intellectual and historical density that made their work worth returning to.

# B-SIDES

## "Pass the Mic (Part 2 — The Skills to Pay the Bills)"

There is something particularly revealing about B-sides — recordings that exist outside the commercial calculus of album sequencing and radio promotion, where artists can pursue ideas for their own sake. "Pass the Mic (Part 2)" is exactly this kind of document: a 1992 B-side from the *Check Your Head* sessions that is simultaneously a showcase for the Beastie Boys' crate-digging expertise, a love letter to the funk tradition, and a demonstration of how deeply the group had absorbed the rhythmic and tonal vocabulary of Black American music from the late sixties through the early seventies.

The Funkadelic sample — drawn from "I Wanna Know if It's Good to You" — is where the track's essential character originates. Funkadelic, the psychedelic funk collective assembled around George Clinton and featuring the guitar work of Eddie Hazel, were among the most important American bands of the late sixties and early seventies, creating a fusion of hard rock, soul, blues, and avant-garde noise that remains genuinely singular in the popular music canon. "I Wanna Know if It's Good to You," from the 1970 album *Funkadelic*, is characteristic of the group's early approach: deeply rooted in the church and the blues, reaching toward the cosmic, and bound together by a groove that is simultaneously loose and inescapable. The hook/riff that the Beastie Boys extract carries everything essential about Funkadelic's aesthetic in concentrated form — a rawness, a quality of something recorded in the heat of the moment by musicians who were playing from the body as much as the mind.

The drum sample from Johnny Jenkins' "I Walk on Guilded Splinters" represents one of the most historically significant choices in the track's architecture. Jenkins is one of those figures who haunts the margins of American music history — a guitarist from Macon, Georgia, who was instrumental in bringing Otis Redding to the attention of Phil Walden. Jenkins himself recorded only one album as a leader: *Ton-Ton Macoute!* in 1970, a sprawling, magnificent document of guitar-driven blues-rock that features some of the most ferocious playing of the era. "I Walk on Guilded Splinters" — an adaptation of Dr. John's 1968 voodoo blues original — is the album's most celebrated track, a seven-minute exploration of swamp blues energy that builds from a slow, hypnotic groove into something genuinely unhinged. The drums on this recording carry the specific physical quality of early-seventies studio recording — they sound like drums in a room, not like drum samples processed through decades of sonic refinement.

The Southside Movement's "Funk Talk" provides the track's other primary hook/riff element. The Southside Movement operated out of Chicago in the early-to-mid seventies as part of the rich ecosystem of regional funk that flourished in cities across America during this period — bands deeply rooted in their local scenes, recording for independent labels, creating music as deeply felt as anything made by nationally celebrated acts. "Funk Talk" is precisely what its title promises: a piece of music entirely in service of the groove, that exists to make people move. The riff that the Beastie Boys extract has the kind of inevitability that characterizes the best funk moments — it sounds like it was discovered rather than composed.

The Buddy Miles Express sample, drawn from "Train" and incorporated as a vocal/lyrical element, adds a further dimension. Miles was a drummer and vocalist of remarkable power who had served as a member of Jimi Hendrix's Band of Gypsys. His work was deeply influenced by Hendrix's fusion of blues, rock, soul, and psychedelia. "Train" features Miles' enormous voice — a voice built for arenas and revival meetings — laying down the kind of proclamatory authority that made him one of the most distinctive vocalists of his era.

By incorporating Miles' voice as a lyrical element rather than simply as a rhythmic sample, the Beastie Boys are placing someone else's words and delivery in direct conversation with their own vocal performance, creating a kind of intergenerational duet. The effect positions the Beastie Boys within a continuum of performance that runs from the psychedelic soul tradition through hip-hop — suggesting that what they are doing in 1992 is not a departure from or commentary on that tradition but a continuation of it by other means.

That this track exists as a B-side — deemed too specific or too dense for inclusion on *Check Your Head*'s main sequence — is itself revealing. The album that did get released was already one of the most sample-literate and historically engaged hip-hop records of its era. The fact that "Pass the Mic Part 2" was considered a step further in that direction speaks to the extraordinary level of ambition the Beastie Boys were operating at during this period, making music that was simultaneously accessible enough to reach a mainstream audience and deep enough to reward the most committed listener with something new on every play.
